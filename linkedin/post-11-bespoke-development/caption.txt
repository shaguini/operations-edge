Two team leaders. Both underperforming. Both flagged by the same KPI scorecard.

On paper, it looked like the same problem.

In practice, they were completely different situations — and diagnosing that difference was the whole job.

One had deep operational experience. Strong technically, respected by peers. But people process compliance was slipping, and the communication style was creating friction in the team that was starting to affect morale and output.

The other was new to the environment. An exceptional communicator — clear, empathetic, natural leadership presence. But lacking technical confidence in a complex part of the operation, and that stress was showing in the shift outcomes.

I designed two completely different interventions:

For the experienced leader: directive coaching using the GROW framework. Feedback tied to observable impact, not intent. Proximity mentoring — catching issues in the moment, in context, where they couldn't be argued away.

For the new leader: supportive mentoring. No correction needed. Review the shift plan together before it starts, then let them run it. Coaching questions, not instructions. Protect the autonomy. Maintain the safety net quietly.

One needed high direction and high challenge. The other needed low direction and high support.

Both improved. Neither would have responded to the same plan.

The learning: fairness in management doesn't mean treating everyone the same. It means knowing what each person actually needs — and being precise enough to deliver it.

The data shows you where the gap is. The conversation shows you why. The intervention has to come from both.
