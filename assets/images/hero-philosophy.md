# Floor Intelligence

A philosophy of industrial precision made visible. This is the aesthetic of systems that work — not celebrated, not decorated, simply documented with the rigour of a master craftsman who has spent decades on the floor.

Form arrives through the orthographic logic of process diagrams: rectangular bays, grid coordinates, flow paths. Nothing decorative. Every line exists because a decision demanded it. Space is structured, not filled — the negative carries as much meaning as the mark. The work must feel like it was labored over for countless hours, each element placed with painstaking deliberation.

Color is restrained to the point of severity. Steel blue — the colour of industrial signage, protective equipment, loading bay doors — dominates. Against deep navy, it carries the authority of a technical specification, not a brand palette. The palette is the product of meticulous calibration: one primary, one accent, near-silence everywhere else.

Rhythm comes from repetition at different scales. Fine grid lines at barely visible opacity. Bolder structural lines at the edges of form. A dominant flow path that moves diagonally through the composition, suggesting force and direction. This layering — from whisper to statement — is the product of deep expertise, the result of knowing exactly how much is enough.

Typography arrives as notation, not communication. Grid references, sector codes, system versions. Characters set in monospace, small, clinical — as if extracted from a technical log. The text does not explain the image. It anchors it in a system. This restraint is master-level execution: knowing what not to say.

The composition must reward sustained attention. At first glance, structure and order. On closer inspection, the data reveals itself: flow nodes, bay references, performance traces. The work is meticulous — every detail perfect, every spacing deliberate, as if produced by someone at the absolute top of their field who would never settle for approximate.
